Why Price Falls When Everyone's Buying

If demand is so high, why does the price sometimes fall? Because we reason about price in a straight line, and a fixed-supply asset does not move in a straight line. Most of the coins are not for sale at any price. The few that are decide the number on the screen – until, one day, they run out.

I.

The straight-line trap

More demand, higher price – until it isn't

We carry a simple model in our heads: more buyers means a higher price, and the line climbs smoothly. For most goods that intuition is roughly right, because supply quietly expands to meet demand. Coffee gets dear, more coffee gets planted. The line bends, but it bends gently.

Bitcoin breaks the model in two places. First, supply cannot expand – it is fixed at twenty-one million, with new coins arriving on a schedule no amount of demand can hurry. Second, and less obvious, most of the existing coins are simply not for sale, at any price you are likely to see this year. They sit in cold storage, in the wallets of people who treat them as a multi-generational inheritance, in the reserves of companies whose entire strategy is never to sell. So the price you watch tick up and down is not the price of all the bitcoin in the world. It is the price of the small, restless sliver that happens to be willing to trade today.

The number on the screen is the price of the sliver,
not the price of the mass.

II.

The margin

Why heavy buying can coincide with a falling price

Here is the part that feels like a paradox and isn't. In the short run, price can fall while serious money is accumulating hard. The two facts live in different layers of the market.

Price is set at the margin – by whoever is willing to sell into the bid right now, not by the silent majority who would not part with a coin under a million dollars. When a long-dormant whale finally distributes, or an early holder from a decade ago quietly sells a tranche into the new institutional demand – a kind of silent IPO, an offering with no prospectus – those coins meet the buyers and the clearing price can sag, even as the buyers are vacuuming up everything they can. Add leveraged traders forced out of positions, and the housekeeping of ETF authorized participants shuffling coins in and out to keep funds balanced, and you get plenty of short-run selling pressure layered on top of relentless accumulation.

So accumulation does not announce itself in the price. It shows up first in who holds the coins – in the slow migration of supply from sellers to holders who will never sell. The price chart is the noise. The ownership chart is the signal. Roughly seventy-two to seventy-five percent of all bitcoin hasn't moved in over six months; long-term holders command somewhere near sixty-eight percent of supply; exchange balances have fallen by about half since late 2024.1 None of that is visible in any single day's red candle.

Accumulation hides in the ledger of ownership
long before it ever reaches the ticker.

III.

The non-linearity

A fixed supply does not re-price gradually

Now run the migration forward. Committed buyers keep absorbing coins: corporate treasuries (some locking them up to earn yield through instruments like STRC), spot ETFs already holding around one and a half million coins,2 sovereigns testing the water, and individuals who simply will not sell below seven figures. Every one of them takes float off the table – permanently.

For a while, nothing dramatic happens to the price. The marginal sellers are still there; the squeeze is invisible because there is still a sliver willing to trade. But the float is finite, and it is shrinking. A fixed-supply asset does not respond to a supply squeeze in a straight line. There comes a point – and it arrives without warning – where the coins demanded at the current price exceed the coins anyone will sell at that price. At that moment the quantity cannot adjust. It is fixed. The buyers cannot conjure more supply, and the holders will not provide it. The only variable left that can move is price, and so it moves – suddenly, not gradually, because the pressure that built quietly for months releases all at once.

This is the difference between a market where supply is a knob you can turn and a market where supply is a wall. Against a knob, demand pushes and the knob gives a little. Against a wall, demand pushes and nothing happens – and then the wall is reached, and the entire adjustment that should have been spread over years happens in a span you can measure in days. IV.

The projector

Watch the liquid float drain

A toy model, not a forecast. It tracks one thing: the pool of coins actually available to buy. Each week the committed buyers take their share, a trickle of newly mined coins refills it, and the pool moves toward the pressure point – the week when, at this price, there are no sellers left.

A toy model. Net weekly drain = buying − issuance, held constant; real demand, supply and price all vary. Crucially, a real market re-prices well before the float literally hits zero – rising price coaxes some holders to sell and cools some buying. Read the crossing not as a date but as a pressure point: where quantity can no longer do the adjusting, and price must.

The lesson the model makes physical: hold the buying steady and the float falls in a straight line – but the consequence is anything but straight. For most of the run, nothing visible happens to price. The drama is all stored up, invisible, until the float thins to nothing and the adjustment that quantity refused to make lands entirely on price.

The float falls in a straight line.
The price does not.

V.

The lesson

Falling price, tightening grip

Strip away the day-to-day and a flat principle remains. A red candle tells you what the marginal seller did this afternoon. It tells you almost nothing about the squeeze.

If most holders treat bitcoin the way the readers of this site tend to – as a lifeline meant to outlast them, not to be sold until seven figures and even then only sparingly, often locked up to earn yield rather than spent – then every one of them is a coin permanently subtracted from the float. The pool of sellers gets shallower with each conviction that hardens. A falling price during heavy accumulation is not evidence against the thesis; under a fixed supply it is exactly what the early innings of a squeeze look like. The mistake is to read a smooth-line story onto a market whose defining feature is that it cannot stay smooth.

The fair objection is that "permanently" is too strong – that the wall is not fixed but moves. "Not for sale at any price" really means "not for sale at this price," and a high enough number has always coaxed out coins their owners swore they would never part with; every prior cycle has done exactly that. But notice what the objection concedes rather than refutes: the only thing that summons the locked supply is a sharply higher price – which is the squeeze, not its absence. The wall moving is the wall doing its work. And each cycle that drags the reluctant sellers out tends to leave a little more of the supply in the hands that held through it, so the float that comes back is thinner than the float that left.

A falling price during heavy accumulation
is what the early innings of a squeeze look like.

There is a deliberate version of this and an accidental one, and the accidental one is the stronger. The deliberate version is a matter of timing: a patient accumulator quietly absorbs coins while supply is loose, knows when enough has been locked away in hands that will not sell, and only then takes the reins off – letting price find the level the scarcity always implied. But a squeeze needs no conductor. When enough players are doing the same thing at once – independently, under the radar, and none of them willing to be the one who lets go – the fixed supply forces the outcome on its own. No participant chooses it; no would-be suppressor permits it. The coins are simply not there at today's price, and a market with no sellers left has only one way to resolve. The break arrives not because someone released it, but because the arithmetic overran everyone's restraint at the same moment.

No one has to let go.
At some point the supply decides for them.

Demand pushing on an expandable supply nudges the price. Demand pushing on a wall does nothing – and then, all at once, does everything. The whole argument is in that difference, and the only honest forecast is its shape, not its date.
