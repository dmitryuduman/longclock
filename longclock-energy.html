The Energy Theory of Money

The sharpest objection to digital money is that it is "just numbers" — conjured from nothing, backed by nothing. It is a good objection aimed at the wrong target. The scarcity is anchored to the one thing in the universe that cannot be faked or printed: real energy, really spent.

I.

The objection, taken seriously

"It's just numbers" — and so is everything else

The complaint feels devastating: this money has no substance, nothing you can hold, just entries in a database. But pause on it, and the same charge convicts every modern currency — and misses what actually makes a thing hard to counterfeit.

The dollars in your account are also just numbers in a database; the difference people reach for is that dollars are "backed by the government." But backed by what, exactly? By the government's ability to make more of them at will — which is the opposite of backing. What gives any money its monetary value is not the material it is made of but how hard it is to produce another unit. Gold is valued because finding and refining it costs enormous effort. The question to ask of digital money, then, is not "can I touch it?" but "what does it cost to make one more?" Answer that, and the "just numbers" objection dissolves.

The question was never "can I hold it?"
It is "what does it cost to make one more?"

II.

The anchor

Scarcity welded to the laws of physics

Bitcoin's supply is not protected by a promise or a vault. It is protected by the cost of electricity.

New coins are issued only to those who expend real, measurable computing work — vast amounts of energy, converted to heat, burned in the physical world to earn the right to add the next block. You cannot shortcut it, charm it, or legislate it away; the only way to produce more is to spend more energy, and the system automatically makes the task harder whenever too many try. This is the same logic that made gold sound — value anchored to unavoidable cost — but expressed in the most universal currency there is. A government can print a number. It cannot print a joule. Whatever else it is, money whose creation is gated by thermodynamics is the furthest thing from "conjured from nothing."

A government can print a number.
It cannot print a joule.

Tap the bellows and watch the only way coins are made: not by decree, but by feeding in work until the heat is high enough to forge exactly one. Stop feeding it and nothing appears. There is no other door.

III.

Why it matters now

The one digital thing that stays expensive

We are entering an age where the cost of producing almost anything digital — text, images, voices, code — is collapsing to nothing. Against that tide, a digital object that remains costly to create is a profound anomaly.

Everything software touches gets cheaper to copy until it is effectively free. That is wonderful for art and knowledge and ruinous for anything meant to store value, because value requires scarcity and scarcity requires cost. By tying issuance to energy, bitcoin builds the one digital thing that refuses to get cheaper — a deliberate, unbypassable expense in a world racing toward frictionless abundance. The critics are right that most digital things are conjured from nothing. They have simply mistaken the one exception for the rule. It is not weightless. It is the heaviest digital object ever made — and it had to be, to do money's job.

Not the lightest digital thing.
The heaviest one ever built.

Money has always been a way of storing effort until you need it back. This is the first money that proves the effort was real, in joules, to anyone who cares to check.
