The Debasement Tax

There is a tax you have paid every year of your life, never filed for, and never voted on. It is collected not from your income but from your savings – by quietly making each unit of money worth a little less. It is the oldest trick in statecraft, and the most patient.

I.

The levy no one votes for

A tax that arrives as weather

An honest tax announces itself. It has a rate, a form, a date. The debasement tax has none of these – which is exactly why it has outlasted every honest one.

When a state spends more than it takes, it can raise taxes and face the voters, or it can make more money and face no one. The second path does not feel like a tax. Prices simply drift up; your wages buy a little less; the savings you set aside lose a sliver of their power while you sleep. No one sends a bill. The loss is real, and it is collected most heavily from the people least able to flee it – savers, pensioners, wage-earners holding cash – while those nearest the new money, who receive it first at full value, are spared. It is a transfer dressed as a natural event.

It does not feel like a tax,
which is the entire point.

II.

Old trick, new tools

Emperors shaved the coin; we print the difference

The mechanism is three thousand years old. Only the instrument has changed.

Rome's silver denarius began as nearly pure silver and, emperor by emperor, was quietly cut with base metal until almost no silver remained – a currency hollowed out to pay armies the treasury could not afford. Medieval mints "clipped" coins, shaving the edges and keeping the parings. The trick was always the same: keep the name and the face, reduce the substance, pocket the gap. Modern money needs no shaving – the substance was already removed when the last link to gold was cut, and now the gap is created by keystroke, in quantities a Roman could not have dreamed. The technology improved. The theft did not change.

Keep the name and the face.
Reduce the substance. Pocket the gap.

At a mild-sounding few percent a year, the loss feels like nothing in any single year – and is devastating across a life. That is the cruelty of the thing: it is always small enough to ignore now and always large enough to matter by the end. A century of it has stripped the great reserve currencies of the overwhelming majority of their purchasing power, quietly, with no crisis to point to. The figures here are illustrative of that compounding, not a forecast – but the shape is the lived experience of every saver who ever trusted cash to keep its word.

Small enough to ignore now.
Large enough to matter by the end.

IV.

The one exit

A money with no dilution switch

Every defence against debasement has historically been a flight to something the issuer could not make more of. Land. Gold. Now, something stricter than either.

The reason a fixed-supply asset lands so hard with people who have studied this is that it removes the lever entirely. There is no committee that can vote to make more, no emperor who can shave it, no keystroke that can dilute the holders. Twenty-one million, and the schedule is known to the last coin. You can argue about its price, its volatility, its path – but the one thing it structurally cannot do is the one thing every state currency eventually does. For a saver, that is not a feature among many. It is the whole point of looking for an exit in the first place.

No committee can vote to make more.
That sentence has never been true of money before.

You were never going to be taxed less. The only choice you ever had was what to hold while it happened – and for the first time, one of the options cannot be quietly cut behind your back.
